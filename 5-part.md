第一部分：
PART 1
Desired Results
What should students learn or be able to do by the end of the lesson?
This tells the AI what the lesson is really aiming for. A clear learning goal helps the AI stay focused instead of generating random activities.
e.g. “Students will be able to identify the main idea of a short text and support it with two details.”

PART 2
Learner & Context
Who are your students, and what teaching situation should the AI know about?
Include details like grade level, language proficiency, class size, prior knowledge, or other important classroom conditions.
e.g. “I teach an ESL class of 20 middle school students at WIDA level 3(Developing).”

PART 3
Evidence of Learning
How will students show that they met the goal?
This helps the AI design a lesson with a meaningful check for understanding, instead of activities that feel disconnected from the goal.
e.g. “Include a short exit ticket where students identify the main idea and give two supporting details.”

PART 4
Instructional Plan
What kind of lesson flow, activities, or supports should the AI include?
You can describe the teaching approach, sequence, grouping, and scaffolds you want in the lesson.
e.g. “Start with teacher modeling, then partner practice, then independent work. Include sentence frames and one guided example.”

PART 5
Output Requirements
What exactly should the AI generate, and how should it be organized?
This tells the AI what final product you want, such as a lesson plan, worksheet, bilingual support, or table format.
e.g. “Create a 40-minute lesson plan with objective, warm-up, guided practice, pair activity, exit ticket, and materials list. Format it as a table.”

以下是更详细的explanation


1. Desired Results and Standards
Teacher-facing definition
 Describe what students should learn in this lesson. State the knowledge, skill, or language use students should demonstrate, and include standards or curriculum goals when relevant.
Why this improves AI output
 When the goal is explicit, the AI can generate a lesson with a clearer focus instead of producing a generic activity list. Real lesson-planning systems and prompt libraries commonly ask for topic, objective, and standards because these inputs strongly shape the usefulness of the final plan.
What learners should type
What students will learn
What students will be able to do
Lesson topic / unit goal
Standard or curriculum target, if needed
Example learner input
 “Students will be able to identify the main idea and support it with two details in a grade 6 informational text. This lesson supports our reading comprehension unit on nonfiction.”
Common mistake
 Writing a teacher action instead of a student learning result.
 Bad example: “Teach main idea.”
 Better: “Students will identify the main idea and explain two supporting details.”

2. Assessment Evidence and Success Criteria
Teacher-facing definition
 Explain how the AI should show whether students met the goal. Include the task students will do, what counts as success, and any check-for-understanding, exit ticket, discussion response, or short assessment you want built into the lesson.
Why this improves AI output
 Without assessment evidence, AI often generates activities that feel engaging but are weakly connected to learning goals. Research on lesson planning with LLMs shows that assessment ideas are one of the areas where teachers find AI useful, and structured lesson-planning tools often make assessment an explicit part of the generation process.
What learners should type
How students will show learning
What the teacher will look for
What “good enough” performance means
Informal or formal assessment format
Example learner input
 “Include a short exit ticket where students write the main idea and two supporting details from a short paragraph. Success means the main idea is accurate and at least two details clearly support it.”
Common mistake
 Only asking for “an assessment” without saying what evidence matters.
 Bad example: “Add an assessment.”
 Better: “Add a 3-minute exit ticket that checks whether students can identify the main idea and justify it with details.”

3. Learner Profile and Teaching Context
Teacher-facing definition
 Describe who the learners are and what classroom conditions matter. Include grade level, language proficiency, prior knowledge, common challenges, class size, time available, and any important learner differences.
Why this improves AI output
 LLM-generated lesson plans become more relevant when the learner profile is clear. Studies with teachers and teacher candidates show that personalization and contextual detail improve perceived usefulness, while vague prompts tend to produce overly generic lessons.
What learners should type
Grade / age group
English level or proficiency
Prior knowledge
Class size
Time limit
Learner needs, variability, or constraints
Example learner input
 “This is for a grade 7 ELS class of 28 students. Most students read near grade level, but several need sentence frames and vocabulary support. The lesson is 40 minutes.”
Common mistake
 Giving only grade level and nothing else.
 Bad example: “Grade 7 class.”
 Better: “Grade 7 ELS, mixed reading levels, 40 minutes, several students need vocabulary scaffolds.”

4. Instructional Sequence and Scaffolds
Teacher-facing definition
 Tell the AI how the lesson should unfold. Include the kind of activities you want, the teaching approach, grouping structure, sequence of steps, and any supports such as modeling, guided practice, sentence frames, discussion prompts, or differentiation.
Why this improves AI output
 If you do not specify the instructional flow, AI often fills the gap with generic teacher-centered steps. Research on structured lesson-planning systems shows that output quality improves when the planning process makes lesson structure explicit rather than leaving the sequence entirely to the model.
What learners should type
Activity sequence
Individual / pair / group work
Discussion or practice type
Needed scaffolds
Differentiation or support moves
Example learner input
 “Start with a short teacher model, then move to partner practice, and end with independent exit ticket work. Include sentence frames and one guided example before students work on their own.”
Common mistake
 Listing an activity but not the learning pathway.
 Bad example: “Include a discussion activity.”
 Better: “Model once, then partner discussion with prompts, then independent written response.”

5. Generation Specifications and Deliverables
Teacher-facing definition
 State exactly what you want the AI to generate and how it should look. Include the format, lesson components, length, tone, materials, language support, and any required sections such as objective, warm-up, guided practice, assessment, or homework.
Why this improves AI output
 This is the part that turns pedagogical intent into a usable AI request. Real lesson-planning applications do not rely on goals alone; they also structure what the final deliverable should contain. The clearer the requested format and components, the more usable and classroom-ready the output becomes.
What learners should type
What artifact to generate
Required sections
Length / time
Tone / style
Bilingual or language support needs
Materials or resource limits
Example learner input
 “Generate a 40-minute lesson plan with objective, warm-up, teacher model, partner activity, exit ticket, and materials list. Use a clear teacher-friendly format and include bilingual vocabulary support in English and Chinese.”
Common mistake
 Requesting a lesson plan without specifying the final product clearly enough.
 Bad example: “Make a lesson plan.”
 Better: “Generate a 40-minute lesson plan with warm-up, modeling, guided practice, assessment, and bilingual vocabulary list.”
